                         SECTION 2: ELDER CABIN VISITS
================================================================================

CABIN 1 - NOHKUM (GRANDMOTHER)
Location: The Kitchen Table
Teachings:
- Beadwork patterns and their meaning
- Each flower connects to the next, like families connect
- Metis as a people of connection (French and Cree, Scottish and Dene)
- Every bead has its place, every pattern has meaning
Math: Greatest Common Factor using bead groupings

CABIN 2 - UNCLE GABRIEL
Location: The Workshop
Teachings:
- Red River Cart construction (no nails, no metal - just wood and rawhide)
- "The Singing Carts" - could be heard for miles
- Buffalo hunt traditions - hundreds of families traveling together
- Balance in life, balance in wheels
Math: Difference of Squares (factoring)

CABIN 3 - FIDDLER PIERRE
Location: The Cabin
Teachings:
- The Red River Jig ("Scottish footwork, Cree soul, French fiddle")
- Saturday night community gatherings
- Music as mathematics (rhythm, harmony, patterns)
- Children falling asleep on coats while adults jigged until sunrise
Math: Factoring Trinomials

CABIN 4 - AUNTIE ANNIE
Location: The Garden
Teachings:
- Women as keepers of the seeds
- Transition from buffalo hunting to agriculture
- Knowledge passed from mother to daughter
- "The land will feed us if we learn her ways"
- Plants growing in curves (parabolas in nature)
Math: Evaluating Quadratic Functions

CABIN 5 - ELDER JOSEPH
Location: The Sacred Fire
Teachings:
- The visiting way (Keeoukaywin)
- Fire gatherings lasting three days
- Smoke rising in circles, carrying prayers upward
- Medicine Wheel - all directions connected, all times connected
- Seasons, generations, sun and moon travel in circles
- Offering of sage
Math: Equation of a Circle

================================================================================
                     SECTION 3: MEDICINE GARDEN PLANTS
================================================================================

SWEETGRASS (Wihkaskwa / Le Foin d'Odeur)
- "The hair of Mother Earth"
- Braided (three strands = mind, body, spirit)
- Used for cleansing the spirit and calming the mind
- Attracts positive energy and kind spirits
- Sweet vanilla-like scent
- Used after sage to invite good things in
Uses: Blessing, Prayer, Attracting positivity, Braiding

SAGE (Li Sawj / La Sauge Sacree)
- A women's medicine
- Removes negative energy
- Cleanses the home and the heart
- Grows wild in the dry prairies
- Burned for smudging
Uses: Smudging, Purification, Ceremony, Healing

CEDAR (Li Sedr / Le Cedre)
- Used for protection and tea (bush tea)
- Rich in Vitamin C
- Helped ancestors survive harsh winters
- Cedar baths cleanse body and spirit
- Branches used in ceremony and medicine
Uses: Protection, Cleansing baths, Tea medicine, Ceremony

TOBACCO (Le Tabac)
- The most sacred of all plants
- Never smoked casually - offered in prayer
- Carries prayers to the Creator and spirits
- Given when asking for guidance or giving thanks
Uses: Prayer offerings, Gratitude, Seeking guidance, Sacred gift

SASKATOON BERRY (Li Pwayr)
- Staple food for the Metis
- Used in Pemmican (Pimiihkkaan)
- Sustained travelers on long journeys
- Branches used for arrows
Uses: Food, Pemmican making

WILD ROSE (La Rose Sauvage)
- Found across the prairies
- Rose hips rich in Vitamin C
- Grandmothers made tea and jam from the hips
- Kept families healthy through harsh winters
Uses: Vitamin C source, Winter health, Tea, Jam making

YARROW (L'Herbe a Dindes)
- Powerful healing plant
- Stops bleeding and treats wounds
- Hunters and travelers carried it for emergencies
- Tea helps with fever and stomach troubles
Uses: Wound healing, Stop bleeding, Fever reducer, Digestive aid

                          SECTION 4: RED RIVER CART
================================================================================

Title: La Charette / The Red River Cart

KEY FACTS PRESENTED:
- Constructed entirely of wood and rawhide
- Contained NO iron (iron would break in cold or bend in heat)
- Hardwood joints wrapped in wet "shaganappi" (rawhide)
- Rawhide shrank to bind tighter than nails
- Wheels up to 6 feet high
- Wheels were "dished" (saucer-shaped) to prevent tipping
- Wheels removed to float cart across rivers
- Axles left ungreased (grease would collect dust)
- Created high-pitched squeal heard for miles
- Organized in "brigades" of hundreds
- Carried buffalo meat, pemmican, trade goods
- Created trails that became Western Canada's first highways

                        SECTION 5: FISHING EXPERIENCE
================================================================================

Title: La Sizon di Pwason (The Fishing Season)

FISH TYPES (Michif Names):
- Li Dore - Pickerel (Walleye)
  "The most prized catch. Sharing the first catch of the season brings
   good luck to the community."

- Li Boroshy - Jackfish (Pike)
  "A fierce predator. Often dried and smoked (wiiyas) to preserve it
   for the long winter ahead."

- La Losh - Burbot
  "A freshwater cod caught in deep, cold waters. Its liver is rich in
   vitamins, crucial for health."

CULTURAL NOTES:
- Fishing was central to Metis life along rivers and lakes
- Knowledge of when and where to fish passed through generations
- Fish dried and smoked to preserve for winter

                     SECTION 7: METIS ARCHITECTURE
================================================================================

Title: Li Batiman (The Buildings of the Metis)

BUILDING TYPE 1: RED RIVER FRAME
Michif: Maison en Poteaux
- Blends French-Canadian building techniques with prairie needs
- Vertical posts (poteaux) set into the ground
- Horizontal logs fitted between posts
- "Piece-sur-piece" method
Features listed:
- Vertical posts anchored in ground
- Horizontal logs between posts
- Whitewashed exterior for weather protection
- Steep roof for snow shedding
- Central fireplace for heating

BUILDING TYPE 2: LOG CABIN
Michif: Cabane en Bois Rond
- Notched corners locked logs together (no nails)
- Gaps filled with moss, mud, or buffalo hair mixed with clay
Features listed:
- Notched corner joints (no nails needed)
- Chinked gaps with mud and moss
- Small windows to retain heat
- Sod or bark roof covering
- Earthen floor, sometimes planked

BUILDING TYPE 3: HIVERNANT LODGE
Michif: Lozh d'Hivernement
- Winter camps for quick, warm shelter
- Combined Indigenous tipi elements with European cabin construction
- Wooden frame covered with buffalo hides or canvas
- Banked with snow for insulation
Features listed:
- Portable wooden frame structure
- Buffalo hide or canvas covering
- Central fire pit with smoke hole
- Snow-banked walls for insulation
- Quick assembly for hunting camps

BUILDING TYPE 4: CACHE / STOREHOUSE
Michif: La Cache
- Built on stilts (protection from animals and moisture)
- Some partially buried for temperature control
- Used for storing pemmican, dried meat, and furs
Features listed:
- Raised on posts away from ground
- Small, secure door
- Ventilation gaps for air flow
- Sometimes partially buried
- Holds pemmican, furs, and tools

                 SECTION 13: REMEMBERING & RESILIENCE (MEMORIAL)
================================================================================

Title: Li Mimwayr (Remembering & Resilience)

HISTORICAL CONTENT:
- Residential school system history
- Over a century of operation
- Canadian government and churches partnership
- Explicit goal of eliminating Indigenous cultures, languages, identities
- Metis, First Nations, and Inuit children taken from families

TIMELINE PRESENTED:
- 1870s-1880s: First residential schools established in western Canada
- 1920: Attendance becomes compulsory. Parents face imprisonment for
        keeping children home
- 1960s: "Sixties Scoop" - thousands of Indigenous children placed
         in non-Indigenous foster homes
- 1996: Last federally-run residential school closes
- 2008: Prime Minister Stephen Harper delivers formal apology
- 2015: Truth and Reconciliation Commission releases final report
        with 94 Calls to Action
- 2021: Unmarked graves discovered at former residential school sites

WHAT WAS LOST (as described):
- Language: Children punished for speaking Michif. Many survivors
            never spoke it again. Today Michif is endangered.
- Traditional Knowledge: Skills passed from grandparents to grandchildren
            interrupted (beadwork, medicine, hunting, storytelling)
- Family Bonds: Children grew up without parents, parents without children.
            Trauma of separation echoes through generations.

ELDER QUOTE USED:
"They tried to take our language, our culture, our identity. But they
couldn't take our spirit. We are still here, and we are bringing back
what they tried to destroy."
- Metis Elder (anonymous)

RESILIENCE SECTION:
- Language Revival: Michif programs growing, apps, courses, immersion
- Cultural Celebration: Metis Days, jigging competitions, fiddle festivals,
                       beadwork, sash weaving, traditional crafts
- Political Recognition: Metis Nation fighting for rights, land claims,
                        self-governance

CALLS TO ACTION MENTIONED:
- Truth and Reconciliation Commission's 94 Calls to Action (referenced)
